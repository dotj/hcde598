Assignment 8: Final Project


The final lamp.

Overview

I made a lamp with two layers of patterned lampshades. The inner lampshade rotates in order to create patterns with the outer lampshade.

Ideation and Concept

I wanted to play with light and have layers of moveable lampshades that would combine to create a lamp with dynamic light patterns. I also started the project with the assumption that everything that can go wrong, will go wrong (Murphy's Law)... especially in a lab setting with limited resources for 40+ people, so I intentionally played it safe.

Process

Bill of Materials

  • LED Pendant Lamp from Target.
  • White Posterboard from Bartell's
  • Plastic Rivets from Joshua.
  • Delrin - 1/8" from Amazon
  • 3D Filament from Fluke

Techniques

  1. Moving parts: Lamp base
  2. Laser cutting: Lamp base and lamp shade
  3. 3D Printing: Knobs for the rotating mechanism.
  4. Parametric modelling: Lamp shade

Lamp Base

I designed the lamp base mainly in Onshape. After a couple of iterations, I decided to make it big enough for a cylindrical lampshade design because I found the spherical shape a little too large. A cylindrical shape means I won't need to find a way to build the scaffolding for the paper lamp shade. In addition, the base has a track in the middle that will allow for us to fit in rivets that carry a secondary lamp shade on the inside. I also created 3D printed knobs, which are hollow spheres with a hole at the bottom sized to snap onto the rivet and hold them in place.


OnShape CAD model on the left, and small 3D printed knobs for the plastic rivets on the right.

Lamp Shade

I calculated the required length and width for two lamp shades and used Grasshopper and Rhino to create patterns on the lamp shade. I initially experimented with variable patterns using attractors and dynamic spacing, though I wasn't happy with those patterns. I ended up sticking with a linearly-spaced pattern with a hyperbolic curve.

I used the laser cutter at Fluke which had a slightly different workflow than the Mill machines, but also afforded much better control. Cutting a single sheet resulted in a lot of burn marks, so I cut two pieces together so that the bottom piece would catch most of the burn marks.

I originally planned to also cut out regularly spaced cut strips on the top of the lampshades to bond with the lamp, but forgot before cutting, so I used small strips of paper to fix the shape to the Delrin base. I had planned to try superglue, which seemed to work for bonding leftover delrin pieces, but eventually decided to simply tape it so that I can iterate on the lampshade in the future.


Part of the Grasshopper definition and a few iterations of the lamp shade design.

Results

The lamp was easy to put together and I didn't get caught in the mad laser cutter chaos at the MILL. The end result was simple but satisfying (even if it makes my apartment smell like BBQ). I'm glad I tried using the Fluke machines, because everything worked far better for me. And while I didn't take too many chances with this project, I found that I was much less intimidated by the idea of designing and fabricating something new when I needed it.

The revolving mechanism worked just as expected. The light from the lamp is nicely diffused from far away, and makes a soft, pleasing "flicker" when the inner shade is rotated. I would definitely like to iterate in the future and find a cleaner way to fix the lampshade to the base, as well as design more interesting lampshades.
